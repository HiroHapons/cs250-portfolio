## Reflection

This course gave me hands-on experience with the Scrum-Agile framework by rotating through each team role across the semester. The most valuable lesson was seeing how Agile's iterative structure allows a team to absorb mid-sprint changes, like the shift to wellness travel in Module Five, without losing momentum. As someone pursuing a career in data science and bioinformatics, understanding how development teams operate in Agile environments will be essential for collaborating on software projects in technical workplaces.

## Essential Questions Reflection

**How do I interpret user needs and implement them into a program? How does creating "user stories" help with this?**

Interpreting user needs starts with understanding not just what a user wants to do, but why they want to do it and what outcome they are trying to achieve. User stories make this concrete by forcing you to frame every requirement from the perspective of a specific user type, a specific action, and a specific goal. In the SNHU Travel project, writing user stories like "as a budget-conscious traveler, I want to set a maximum price limit so that I can find destinations within my budget" made it immediately clear what the feature needed to do and what success looked like. Without that framing, a developer might build a price filter that works technically but misses what the user actually needed. User stories also feed directly into acceptance criteria, which define the exact conditions a feature must meet before it is considered done. This connection between the user's goal and the testable outcome is what makes user stories one of the most practical tools in Agile development.

**How do I approach developing programs? What Agile processes do I hope to incorporate into my future development work?**

My approach to developing programs has shifted significantly through this course toward iterative, incremental thinking rather than trying to plan everything upfront. The practice I most want to carry into future work is sprint-based development with short feedback cycles, because it forces early delivery of working software and surfaces problems before they compound. I also want to continue using acceptance criteria as a bridge between requirements and testing, since it eliminates a lot of ambiguity about what "done" actually means. In my bioinformatics and data science work, I plan to apply backlog-style task management to research and analysis projects, breaking large objectives like building a data pipeline or training a model into smaller, testable increments rather than treating them as monolithic efforts.

**What does it mean to be a good team member in software development?**

Being a good team member in software development means communicating proactively, owning your role clearly, and making it easier for others to do their work. In the SNHU Travel project, I saw how much a single unclear requirement or a delayed response from the Product Owner could block the entire team. Good team members do not wait to be asked for information that others need. They also stay aware of how their work connects to the rest of the team, so a developer understands what the tester needs before handing off code, and a tester understands what the Product Owner intended before writing pass/fail criteria. In Agile especially, being a good team member also means being honest in retrospectives about what is not working, because continuous improvement only happens if people are willing to surface problems rather than absorb them quietly.
